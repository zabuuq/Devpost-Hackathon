# Battlestations: Nebula

*Hunt blind. Probe twice. Fire once.*

## A Turbo Pascal ghost, rebuilt for the browser

Forty-odd years back, Jason's dad cobbled together a hidden-fleet space combat game in Turbo Pascal. It ran on a computer that's probably holding up a wobbly table in someone's basement, in a language that's now just a punchline at retro programming meetups. Two players, one floppy disk, and enough tension to make your palms sweat through the keyboard. The game stuck in his head like a splinter, long after the hardware went to silicon Valhalla.

![Command overview](13_command_overview.png)

This is that game, exhumed and reanimated for the browser. Same bones, new century, different flavor of existential dread. The green CRT is gone, replaced by a nebula that looks like a screensaver ate a star. The sound effects are loud enough to make your neighbor's cat file a noise complaint. You still hide your fleet where the other commander can't see it. You still take turns groping around in the cosmic fog. You still lose ships because you guessed wrong about where someone was three turns ago, which is, let's be honest, the only reason anyone plays.

Two commanders. One 80-by-20 grid. Five ships each, lurking in the nebula like introverts at a family reunion. You and your so-called friend pass the same keyboard back and forth, and the universe decides who gets to keep their dignity and who gets a constellation of wreckage markers and a laptop that smells faintly of shame and stale Doritos.

![Probe closeup](11_probe_closeup.png)

## A turn in the fog

Your ships sit on your side of the board, glowing and obedient, little yellow arrows pointing wherever their captains swear is a good idea. The enemy's side is a cloud. You spend energy to poke the cloud with a probe, and for two turns, you can see exactly which flavor of fudgesicle is about to ruin your afternoon: type, facing, shields, armor, maybe a destroyer already rehearsing its villain monologue.

![Probe reveal](07_probe_revealed.png)

Then the lights go out. The probe fades. The nebula hands you a souvenir: a ghost marker, pinned to the map like a passive-aggressive sticky note reading "a ship was here once, good luck, buddy." Ghost markers are permanent. So is regret. Usually, they're both wrong, because the enemy moved three turns ago and you didn't check your notes.

![Move preview](09_move_preview.png)

On a good turn, you peel off a shield with a laser, punch through armor with a missile, and watch the enemy ship blossom into wreckage the nebula couldn't care less about. On a bad turn, you fire into the void, your battle log shrugs, and your friend chuckles as they eat your last chip.

The energy sliders let you split each ship's reactor between shield regen and laser power. Heads up: you will allocate wrong. Everyone allocates wrong. The ones who say they don't are lying or are already vaporized. When you end your turn, the handoff screen swaps commanders, and the whole sorry circus starts over from the other side of the keyboard.

First commander to break all five enemy hulls wins. The other one gets a Victory screen that points at their stats, a laptop so quiet you can hear your own existential dread, and the dignified realization that in the grand ledger of space, you're a rounding error with a high score in disappointment.
